Complaint & Advisor Dashboard

Complaint volume remains high but shows signs of stabilization.
Total complaints have reached 306K, indicating significant operational demand. However, complaint growth has slowed recently, with Q1 complaints declining from 49K to 45K, suggesting early signs of stabilization.
Banking and Consumer Credit drive the majority of complaints.
Banking & Payments (122.9K) and Consumer Credit (117.6K) together account for nearly 80% of total complaints. These categories are the primary drivers of complaint workload and operational pressure.
Finance Linked Services and Credit Cards represent key risk areas.
Finance Linked to Goods and Services (~105K) and Credit Card Accounts (~65K) generate a large share of complaints. These product areas require attention as they significantly contribute to overall complaint levels.
High uphold rate suggests genuine service issues.
The overall uphold rate is 32%, meaning approximately one in three complaints is upheld. Products such as Personal Bank Accounts and Insurance show relatively higher uphold rates, indicating potential operational or service quality gaps.
Advisor workload is highly concentrated.
Workload pressure is highest in the following categories:
Banking and Payments (Workload Index: 165K)
Consumer Credit (Workload Index: 156K)
This concentration increases the risk of delays, reduced efficiency, and advisor overload.

Workforce capacity must align with forecast demand.
Forecasts indicate approximately 278K complaints expected in FY2026. Based on current productivity assumptions, around 45 advisors may be required to manage the expected complaint workload efficiently.

Complaint reduction depends on process improvement.
The relatively high uphold rate suggests that many complaints are linked to genuine service or operational issues. Therefore, reducing complaint volumes will require process and service improvements, not only increased handling capacity.

Note:
This analysis is based on publicly available complaint data. The advisor requirement estimate is derived from analytical assumptions regarding complaint volumes and productivity levels. As a result, the figures should be interpreted as indicative insights rather than exact operational staffing requirements
